"Hippopotamus (William)---ca. 1961–1878 B.C.",
"This statuette of a hippopotamus popularly called William
was molded in faience a ceramic material made of ground
quartz Beneath the blue glaze the body was painted with
lotuses These river plants depict the marshes in which the
animal lived but at the same time their flowers also
symbolize regeneration and rebirth as they close every night
and open again in the morning

The seemingly benign
appearance that this figurine presents is deceptive To the
ancient Egyptians the hippopotamus was one of the most
dangerous animals in their world The huge creatures were a
hazard for small fishing boats and other rivercraft The
beast might also be encountered on the waterways in the
journey to the afterlife As such the hippopotamus was a
force of nature that needed to be propitiated and controlled
both in this life and the next This example was one of a
pair found in a shaft associated with the tomb chapel of the
steward Senbi II at Meir an Upper Egyptian site about thirty
miles south of modern Asyut Three of its legs have been
restored because they were probably purposely broken to
prevent the creature from harming the deceased The hippo was
part of Senbi's burial equipment which included a canopic
box also in the Metropolitan Museum a coffin and numerous
models of boats and food production

The hippo's modern
nickname first appeared in 1931 in a story that was
published in the British humor magazine Punch It reports
about a family that consults a color print of the Met’s
hippo—which it calls William—as an oracle The Met
republished the story the same year in the Museum’s Bulletin
and the name William caught on.",

거대한 하마들은 작은 어선이나 다른 강 배들에게
위협이 되었고 사후 세계로 가는 여정에서도 물길에서
마주칠 수 있었습니다


그래서 하마는 이승과 저승 모두에서 달래고 통제해야

이 하마 조각상은 상이집트 메이르 지역의 관리인
센비 2세의 무덤 예배당과 관련된 수직갱에서 발견된
한 쌍의 조각상 중 하나입니다


메이르는 현대 아스유트에서 남쪽으로 약 30마일 떨어진 곳입니다


하마의 다리 세 개는 고의적으로 부러진 것으로
보이며 이는 하마가 사망자에게 해를 끼치는 것을
막기 위한 것이었을 것입니다


이 하마는 센비의 매장 용품의 일부였는데 여기에는

이 하마의 현대 별명 윌리엄은 1931년 영국의
